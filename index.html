For the 2021–22 season, the Warriors wore a Warriors Origins jersey, which is a modernization of their 1961–62 road uniform. The jersey also commemorates the 75th anniversary of the franchise.

History

the story of legend

1946–1962: Early years in Philadelphia

The Warriors were founded in 1946 as the Philadelphia Warriors, a charter member of the Basketball Association of America. The owners named the team after the Philadelphia Warriors, an old basketball team who played in the American Basketball League in 1925. Led by early scoring sensation Joe Fulks, the team won the championship in the league's inaugural 1946–47 season by defeating the Chicago Stags, four games to one, and won its next one in the 1955–56 season.

1959–1965: The Wilt Chamberlain era

In 1959, the team signed draft pick Wilt Chamberlain. Known as "Wilt the Stilt", he led the team in scoring six times, quickly began shattering NBA scoring records and changed the NBA style of play forever. On March 2, 1962, in a Warrior "home" game played on a neutral court in Hershey, Pennsylvania, Chamberlain scored 100 points against the New York Knicks, a single-game record the NBA ranks among its finest moments.

Prior to the 1963–64 NBA season, the Warriors drafted big man Nate Thurmond to go along with Chamberlain. The Warriors won the Western Division crown that season, but lost the 1964 NBA Finals to the Boston Celtics, four games to one. In the 1964–65 season, the Warriors traded Chamberlain to the Philadelphia 76ers for Connie Dierking, Lee Shaffer, Paul Neumann and $150,000 and won only 17 games.

1965–1978: Thurmond and Barry

In 1965, they drafted Rick Barry in the first round who went on to become NBA Rookie of the Year that season and then led the Warriors to the NBA Finals in the 1966–67 season, losing (four games to two) to Chamberlain's new team that had replaced the Warriors in Philadelphia, the 76ers.

The franchise adopted its brand name Golden State Warriors prior to the 1971–72 season, to suggest that the team represented the entire state of California. The Warriors made the playoffs from 1971 to 1977 except in 1974, and won their first NBA championship on the West Coast in 1974–75. Barry was named Finals MVP.

At 59–23, the Warriors had the league's best record during the 1975–76 season. They were upset, however, by the 42–40 Phoenix Suns in seven games in the Western Conference Finals.

1978–1985: A period of struggles

Due of the loss of key players such as Barry, Wilkes and Thurmond to trades and retirements, the Warriors struggled to put a competitive team on the court from 1978 to 1987 after being one of the NBA's dominant teams in the 1960s and most of the 1970s.

The departure of these players for various reasons symbolized the franchise's futility during this period, as head coach Attles moved up to the front office as general manager in 1980 and the team made several coaching changes. New owners Jim Fitzgerald and Dan Finane finally managed to return the team to respectability by hiring former Cleveland Cavaliers head coach George Karl as head coach in 1986 after selecting St. John's small forward Chris Mullin in the 1985 NBA draft.

1985–1997: The Chris Mullin and "Run TMC" era

The Golden State Warriors had a remarkable moment in 1987 when they faced the Los Angeles Lakers in the Western Conference Semifinals. This game, famously known as the "Sleepy Floyd game," featured an exceptional performance by Floyd, who scored a record-breaking 51 points. His incredible display, including six consecutive field goals in the fourth quarter, propelled the Warriors to victory and sparked a surge of interest in the NBA in the Bay Area.

Following the "Sleepy Floyd game," the Warriors experienced success in the late 1980s and early 1990s under coach Don Nelson and the dynamic trio of Tim Hardaway, Mitch Richmond, and Chris Mullin, known as "Run TMC." However, despite their scoring prowess, the trio's time together was short-lived, and they only managed to win one playoff series. Internal conflicts and subsequent player departures led to a decline in the team's performance and marked a period of rebuilding for the Warriors.

1997–2009: Wilderness years and "We Believe" Warriors

After a series of setbacks, the Golden State Warriors hired P. J. Carlesimo as head coach for the 1997–98 season, replacing Rick Adelman. However, their troubles continued as Latrell Sprewell's altercation with Carlesimo during a team practice led to his season-long suspension. The incident tarnished the team's image, and general manager Garry St. Jean declared that Sprewell would never play for the Warriors again. The team faced criticism for management decisions and their inability to make significant progress in the competitive Western Conference.

Under the leadership of Chris Mullin as Executive Vice President of Basketball Operations and with Don Nelson returning as head coach in the 2006–07 season, the Warriors rallied with the slogan "We Believe." They made a memorable playoff run, becoming the first No. 8 seed to defeat a No. 1 seed in a seven-game series by eliminating the Dallas Mavericks. Despite their postseason success, subsequent seasons proved challenging with injuries, roster changes, and the departure of key players hindering their progress. The team fell short of making the playoffs in the 2008–09 season with a 29–53 record.

2009–present: The Stephen Curry era

In 2009, the Warriors drafted Stephen Curry with the seventh overall pick, and he quickly emerged as a future superstar. However, the team struggled with injuries and finished the season with a 26-56 record. Curry finished second in the NBA Rookie of the Year voting.

In the 2010 NBA draft, the Warriors selected Ekpe Udoh, and they made several offseason moves, including trading for David Lee and signing Dorell Wright. The team was sold to new owners, Peter Guber and Joe Lacob, for a record $450 million.

Despite the struggles, the Warriors made significant moves in the 2012 offseason. They drafted Harrison Barnes, Festus Ezeli, and Draymond Green, and signed Andre Iguodala. Curry also agreed to a contract extension. The team started the 2012-2013 season strong, earning their first playoff berth in six years and finishing with a 47-35 record. They defeated the Denver Nuggets in the first round but lost to the San Antonio Spurs in the second round.

In the 2013-2014 season, the Warriors improved further, finishing with a 51-31 record. They secured a playoff spot for the second consecutive year but were eliminated by the Los Angeles Clippers in the first round. Curry and Thompson continued to set records for three-point shooting.

Following the season, Mark Jackson was fired as head coach, and Steve Kerr was hired as his replacement. Kerr's arrival marked the beginning of a historic period for the Warriors.
